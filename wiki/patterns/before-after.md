## How it actually worked (from real teardowns)
- _(tech and SaaS)_ The saves (17,690) disproportionate to comments (397) reveal the dominant behavior: viewers are bookmarking this as a purchase reference, not engaging conversationally. The mechanism is a heat-wave timing wedge — the video launched a visceral, time-sensitive fear ('hot summer,' 'stay safe') and offered a cheap, immediately purchasable solution via TikTok Shop. The physical condensation proof removes skepticism that text or specs never could. People saved it to buy later or send to someone suffering in the heat — it functions as a product recommendation artifact, not entertainment
- _(home and cleaning)_ The caption and the video content are describing two entirely different products and scenarios — the caption says 'couch' and the video says 'car interior' — yet both promise the same emotional payoff: rescuing something valuable from irreversible damage with a single product. This mismatch almost certainly triggers comments from confused viewers ('wait is this for the couch or the car?'), which the algorithm reads as engagement, and simultaneously the dual-context framing makes the video saveable by two distinct audiences at once: car owners and couch owners. The saves-to-likes ratio (~24%) is unusually high, indicating viewers are banking it for personal future use, not just approving it.
- _(ABG beauty)_ The shares and saves are driven by identification, not aspiration. The creator accidentally demonstrates the ABG look as *accessible* rather than expert — she lost her beauty blender, she is unsure about her shade match, the blush does not show on camera. This lowers the perceived skill barrier for viewers who want the aesthetic but feel excluded by polished tutorials. The 'is this actually good?' ambiguity makes people tag friends and save it to benchmark against their own routine, not to copy a flawless technique.
