## How it actually worked (from real teardowns)
- _(tech and SaaS)_ The save rate (31,100 saves on 13.8M views) is the dominant signal here — this video functioned as a 'bookmark-to-buy' item rather than pure entertainment. The combination of a concrete, demonstrable physical problem (bulk blocking hand placement), a visible mechanical solution (flat profile + flip stand), and an explicit scarcity cue ('sale looks like it's ending soon') creates a purchase-intent loop that people save to return to. Shares likely propagated through the 'send this to someone who does this' behavior — the scenario of charging while watching is universal enough that viewers had an immediate, named person to forward it to.
- _(hair)_ The share and save ratio (5,580 shares + 5,780 saves against 21,500 likes = roughly 53% of likers bookmarked or forwarded it) indicates this was treated primarily as a utility artifact — specifically a product to send to gray-haired relatives or save for personal purchase later. The pain point (salon cost and appointment friction) is universal across age groups, but the solution maps almost perfectly onto an older demographic that is underserved on short-form video yet has high purchase intent; younger viewers forward it to parents or grandparents, which is what multiplied distribution far outside the creator's 1,821-person audience
- _(home and cleaning)_ The video solves a hidden anxiety most homeowners and homebuyers have (sound transfer between rooms) but have never seen addressed at the construction level — it reframes a finished-home problem as a framing-stage decision, making it shareable as 'what to ask your builder' ammunition; the save rate (~1% of views) confirms people are bookmarking it as a reference, not just consuming it, which signals durable utility rather than entertainment
